Section 1
The Notes
Beginner

Western music uses 12 distinct pitches that repeat in higher and lower registers called octaves. Seven have letter names — A B C D E F G — and five are sharps or flats that sit between them.

Natural notes
A B C D E F G
7 notes — the white keys on a piano
Sharp (♯)
One step up
C♯ is one semitone above C
Flat (♭)
One step down
D♭ is one semitone below D
Enharmonics
Same pitch
C♯ and D♭ are the same key
The 12 semitones in order
C · C♯/D♭ · D · D♯/E♭ · E · F · F♯/G♭ · G · G♯/A♭ · A · A♯/B♭ · B — then the pattern repeats one octave higher.

An octave is the distance between two notes with the same letter name — for example C3 and C4. The number refers to the octave register: middle C on a standard piano is C4. Each octave up doubles the frequency of the note.

Section 2
The Keyboard
Beginner

A standard piano has 88 keys spanning over 7 octaves. The repeating pattern of 7 white and 5 black keys makes every octave identical in layout. Recognising the pattern is the fastest way to find any note.

The black keys appear in groups of 2 and 3. This is your map: C is always to the left of a group of 2 black keys. Find any group of 2 black keys — the white key immediately left is C.

Finding Middle C
Middle C (C4) sits roughly in the centre of a full-size 88-key piano — just to the left of the two black keys nearest the middle. On a 61-key keyboard it is the C closest to the centre. In Piano Coach, all songs are labelled with octave numbers so C4 always means middle C.

Beginner

Relative major & minor
Every major scale shares all its notes with a relative minor starting on the 6th degree. C major and A minor use exactly the same notes — the difference is which note feels like "home". This is why A minor is called the relative minor of C major.

Intervals
Intermediate

An interval is the distance between two notes measured in semitones. Knowing intervals lets you build chords, recognise melodies by ear, and understand why certain note combinations sound the way they do.

Consonance vs. Dissonance
Consonant intervals (unison, 3rds, 5ths, 6ths, octave) sound stable and pleasing on their own. Dissonant intervals (2nds, 7ths, tritone) create tension that the ear wants resolved — this push and pull is what gives music its forward motion.
Section 5
Chords
Intermediate

A chord is three or more notes played simultaneously. Triads — three-note chords — are the foundation of virtually all Western harmony. They are built by stacking intervals of thirds above a root note.

Major
Root + M3 + P5
C major: C E G
Bright, happy sound. Most common chord type.
Minor
Root + m3 + P5
A minor: A C E
Darker, more emotional than major.
Diminished
Root + m3 + d5
B dim: B D F
Tense, unstable — used for drama.
Augmented
Root + M3 + A5
C aug: C E G♯
Mysterious, dreamlike quality.
Major 7th
Root + M3 + P5 + M7
Cmaj7: C E G B
Rich, jazzy and warm.
Dominant 7th
Root + M3 + P5 + m7
G7: G B D F
Strong pull toward resolution.
Minor 7th
Root + m3 + P5 + m7
Am7: A C E G
Smooth, used heavily in jazz and pop.
Suspended 4th
Root + P4 + P5
Csus4: C F G
Ambiguous — neither major nor minor.
The I–IV–V progression
The three most important chords in any key are built on the 1st, 4th, and 5th scale degrees. In C major that is C – F – G. This progression underlies hundreds of songs across every genre from classical to pop to blues.
Section 6
Rhythm & Time
Beginner

Rhythm is how music is organised in time. A steady pulse called the beat divides music into equal units. The time signature tells you how many beats are in each measure and what note value counts as one beat.

44
Common time
4 beats per measure. Quarter note = 1 beat. Most common in pop, classical, and folk.
34
Waltz time
3 beats per measure. Strong–weak–weak feel. Used in waltzes and many classical pieces.
68
Compound duple
6 eighth notes per measure, felt in 2 groups of 3. Flowing, lilting character.
24
March time
2 beats per measure. Strong–weak feel. Used in marches and polkas.

Note durations in 4/4 time:

Whole note
4 beats
Fills an entire measure in 4/4
Half note
2 beats
Two per measure in 4/4
Quarter note
1 beat
The basic pulse in 4/4 — BPM counts these
Eighth note
½ beat
Two per quarter note — often felt as "and"
Sixteenth note
¼ beat
Four per quarter note — fast passages
Dotted note
× 1.5
A dot adds half the note's value — dotted quarter = 1.5 beats
BPM in Piano Coach
BPM (beats per minute) sets the speed. At 60 BPM each quarter note lasts exactly 1 second. At 120 BPM it lasts 0.5 seconds. The rhythm trainer in Piano Coach measures how closely your note timings land on the expected beat positions.

Key Signatures
Intermediate

A key signature tells you which notes are consistently sharp or flat throughout a piece, so composers don't have to write accidentals on every single note. It appears at the start of each staff, right after the clef.

C major / A minor
No sharps or flats
All white keys — the easiest starting point
G major / E minor
1 sharp
F♯ — used in many folk and classical pieces
D major / B minor
2 sharps
F♯ C♯
F major / D minor
1 flat
B♭ — very common in orchestral music
B♭ major / G minor
2 flats
B♭ E♭ — common in wind music
E♭ major / C minor
3 flats
B♭ E♭ A♭ — Moonlight Sonata uses C♯ minor
The Circle of Fifths
Moving clockwise around the Circle of Fifths adds one sharp each step. Moving anticlockwise adds one flat. Adjacent keys on the circle are very closely related — pieces often modulate between them. C major sits at the top with no accidentals; C♯ major (7 sharps) and C♭ major (7 flats) sit at the bottom.

Why dynamics matter for beginners
Many beginners play everything at the same volume. Adding even simple loud/soft contrast immediately makes a piece more musical. Start by playing the first note of each phrase a little louder — this single change creates natural phrase shaping without learning every marking.
